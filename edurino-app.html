The Problems

After reviewing Edurino's current onboarding experience I identified a couple of pain points which could discourage or hinder users from completing the setup process and signing up.

For one thing I found it to be too adult focused since the app is made for children. I felt that the process could be a lot more fun for both the child and the adult if they would do it together.

The first screen when opening the app promises an “adventure” together with Edurino, but as users proceed, they just click through a number of white screens with text.

The overall design of the onboarding process also lacks consistency, with varying border radii, fonts, and spacing between elements. This inconsistency makes navigation more difficult and detracts from the app's professionalism.

Since Edurino advertises a “personalized” learning experience, I wanted the user to feel that the app would adapt to their personality and needs.

Lastly, users are more likely to complete the process if they know from the start how long it will take and which step they are currently on.

Solutions

My ideas

First of all, I overhauled the design by taking inspiration from Edurino's existing color palette (such as the purple) and design philosophy (like rounded edges and solid color icons). This helps the user to navigate and enjoy the process and is also more child friendly.

To keep the child, who is now also involved in this process, entertained, I introduced an Edurino figurine to each screen that forms a connection with the child and guides them.

To make good on that promise of an adventure, I redesigned the whole experience to feel like traveling through a world, conveyed through a landscape in the background that moves as the user progresses.

Lastly, I added a progress bar to give the user an orientation of where in the process they are right now.

Since I want the parent and child to do the whole process together, they will take turns completing screens.

Project Takeaways

Always thinking about who the target audience will (or could) be

It's easy to stick to the norms, such as having only the adults do the onboarding process, but often it can be beneficial to reevaluate them and adjust them to individual cases. In this case including the child in the target audience resulted in what I believe is a better onboarding experience for both the child and the parent.

Giving the users information and control

No one really likes onboarding, they just want to use the app already. To encourage users it is important to give them information about what they're doing like the time it will take or the number of steps. Providing alternatives and shortcuts motivates those who lack time or patience and gives every user a feeling of control over this process.

Getting the layers right

Using layers to prioritize information on the screen while also drawing the user in with an intriguing design. A screen can easily become too cluttered with different layers, but when done right it can immerse the viewer in the experience.
