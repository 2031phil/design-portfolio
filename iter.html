Background

In 2024 I received the offer to work as a UX/UI designer on the Iter project by a young, swedish entrepreneur, who wanted to improve the way people were discovering new places, at home and abroad. She had already developed a prototype of such an app which, however, lacked a well designed user interface. It was my task to improve the usability and design of the prototype so that it could be released as a real app.

The idea behind Iter is to provide the user with personalized recommendations for points of interest (POIs) and events based on what they like. In order to do so the user first completes a short survey based on which the app then calculates match percentages for each POI nearby.

Below you can see what the previous design looked like. A simple login screen, preference survey, map screen and a screen allowing users to contribute to the Iter’s map by adding POIs.

What was still missing was an onboarding, a POI-details screen, app and profile settings, and POI recommendations. Thus, along with a redesign of the existing screens I was going to have to design these missing screens as well and connect them all together to create a seamless user experience.

Competitive Analysis

Google Maps

Google Maps is a leading map-based travel application that features a bottom navigation bar with three primary items and a prominent search bar at the top. The app effectively draws users in through a range of engaging map filter options and visually appealing thumbnails for POIs, making exploration intuitive and visually rich. However, its interface leans toward a more cluttered and adaptable design, characterized by frequent use of accent colors and a heavy reliance on icons. This icon-centric approach, while functional, can contribute to visual overload, especially for new users navigating the platform for the first time.

Tripadvisor

Tripadvisor is a card-based travel app that emphasizes content discovery through visually engaging photos, often used to highlight destinations, accommodations, and experiences. The interface features a bottom navigation bar with five items and a top search bar, supplemented by an additional search option within the navigation menu. Unlike more icon-driven platforms, Tripadvisor adopts a simpler, cleaner design with a consistent monochromatic color scheme, resulting in a more streamlined and approachable user experience. The app leans heavily on text to deliver detailed information, making it particularly suited for users who prefer in-depth content over quick visual cues.

Structuring Iter

Like in Google Maps, I wanted to make the map the home screen of the app. This allows the user to have a sense of orientation right from the start by finding himself on the map. Furthermore, it is what many users would expect when opening a travel app. From here, the user can reach any other screen in one or two clicks at most.

Besides the map/home screen, there are two other main screens: The “For You” page, which shows POIs as cards, and the “Contribute (Add POI)” page, which lets users add places to the map or write reviews of POIs.

After a couple of discussions with the client, we decided to drop the search function for now, since that would take a lot more time to develop. I also discontinued the idea of swiping right or left from the map screen to open the For You or Add POI screen since swiping was already used to move the map around.

Design Decisions

Based on the already existing Iter logo, I decided to use blue as my primary color. On the For You page I went for a lighter, more turquoise blue to visually separate it from the rest of the app and thus invite the user to engage with it more. As a background, I decided to go for a gradient to make the design less flat and more adventurous. Lastly, I reserved purple as the accent color for my percentage match indicator, to highlight its importance.

Speaking of the percentage match indicator, I knew it would be important to do something recognizable and unique with it since this was what set Iter apart from its competitors: Transparently showing its users why something gets recommended to them. To portray that, I designed a circle whose completeness proportionally corresponds to the match percentage for a given POI.

The font I chose for Iter is Raleway by Google since it is very readable, modern, different and free to use. The icons are from Apple’s SF family since it will be an iOS app.

The client was also interested in having a unique rating system in the app that would be fun for the users to use. My immediate thought was to use emojis, which turned out to be an idea the client was very happy with and which made the app unique in yet another way. These emojis also play a vital role in the setup of the app where users rate different topics to calibrate the recommendation algorithm.

Project Takeaways

Design Variations

To reach a final design that’s beautiful, innovative and effective, it’s necessary to design multiple variations of important screens and points in the user journey. This allows you to compare designs and take the best of each variation.

Frequent Communication with the Client

Meeting frequently with the stakeholders of a project (in this case my client) is the only way to truly figure out what the client wants while wasting as little of your time as possible designing things that will get scrapped in the end or going down a wrong path.

Jakob’s Law

I paid quite a lot attention in this project to using already established design patterns where it benefitted the design. Looking through suggested POIs on the For You page, for example, is like swiping right or left in a dating app. Opening a submenu (like the Settings screen) shows this layered page effect that I discovered is quite common among iOS applications. Things like these will ultimately decrease the time and effort for users to learn how to use a new app.
